Chain Reaction

Source Code
Demo (Warning: Sound included)
HTML5, CSS3, JavaScript
Gameplay

Place the target anywhere on the canvas, and capture as many balls as possible. At any given level, you need to capture a minimum number of balls in order to advance to the next level. The larger the chain reaction you create, the more points you score!

Demo
Design

Some of my key design features were:

  • Requires minimal effort: player only makes 1 move per round
  • Fun but not too addictive: limit number of levels to 5 to reduce the time of a given playing session
  • Vibrance: integrate colorful designs and sound effects in the game
  • Blend of luck and strategy: balls will move randomly on the screen to add an element of luck and spontaneity, but player has to strategically position target to capture as many balls as possible

SumRise

Source Code
Demo
NodeJS, Express, jQuery, PostgreSQL
Inspiration

I created this app hoping to increase my productivity in both reading and writing. I'm a huge fan of GDrive and GDocs, and SumRise is my attempt at recreating that, with the added feature of summarizing web articles and textual content.

How it works
  • For web articles, enter the URL of your web article. The text summarizer will then parse the main textual content from the web page, and generate a summarized version. It also automatically saves the article title for you.
  • For writing entries, copy and paste your writing content, and title your writing entry if you wish to. Likewise, the text summarizer will then summarize your writing content.
  • You can create folders to organize your articles/writing entries.
  • Everything is saved in the cloud: you can also edit and delete your articles/writing entries/folders.

2. Generated Summary + Title
3. User enters writing content + title
4. Edit Article
5. Edit Writing Content
6. Create folders
7. Organize into folders
8. Track your progress!

3rd Place Overall
Manish Nair, Clement Lee, Lim Zhao Wei
Background

My friends and I recently took part in a hackathon called 'Park of the Future' in Singapore. The theme was on envisioning a model for reinventing business parks of the future, to improve the experiences and operations of both tenants and landlords.

We won the ‘Refining Residents’ Experience’ Challenge and came in 3rd overall! It was a ton of fun, and I learnt a lot as a rookie hacker.

Our proposal

We talked to a few stakeholders, and they told us that they want a vibrant space that attracts innovative professionals, a co-space that fosters co-creation and synergies. We gathered that what the business park of today is missing is this community, and the spontaneous burst of life.

CookieTalk is a platform for tenants to organize and attend spontaneous, pop-up events within business parks. We want to create a culture of ground-up self-organisation: with CookieTalk, any resident can create their own event — and these are meant to be spontaneous, pop-up events — not centrally organised workshops or conferences.

How it works
1. Create an event
2. Event has been successfully created
3. Clicking 'fly to event' navigates the user to the event location on the map.
Click on the map marker to see the details of the event.
4. View a list of all events, including yours
5. Indicate your interest in an event
6. Edit/Delete your events
Previous Next
Next Steps

We're looking to integrate "interest groups" to better connect users / residents to each other. In the longer term, we're looking to expand our target demographic to the general public and nearby institutions.

Additionally, we think that data generated from CookieTalk may be useful for higher management in business parks, giving insight into behavioural trends among residents.

Source Code (Back End)

Ruby on Rails, React, PostgreSQL
Jian Hao Tan, Isa Tokunaga
Background

In Southeast Asia where I come from, K-pop has taken the region by storm over the past two decades, and has amassed an enormous fanbase.

Many K-pop (Korean pop) music shows in South Korea have a tradition of crowning a “winning artist of the week,” determined by a chart system that focuses heavily on digital sales, retail sales, and social media popularity. For example, here's the chart system breakdown for the M! Countdown Weekly Music Awards:

M! Countdown Chart System

Inspiration

My teammate Isa's friend, a huge K-pop fan, complained that she wanted to track her idol groups’ standing in the days leading up to the winning announcements, but had to manually visit the different sources of information, a process she found tedious and troublesome. She added purchasing albums is significantly difficult for non-Korean K-pop fans like her due to the language barrier and the fact that they are not physically in Korea.

We did some further research, and found that these music award shows tend to exploit the psychology of fandom to build buzz and create hype. According to Billboard, the chart systems' heavy weightage on Digital / Physical Sales as well as Social Media Points has given rise to a movement of "breaking records" whereby some fans go to the extent of repeatedly buying albums both online and in retail stores.

We identified that there is a lack of an aggregator for the chart system breakdown statistics, and thus the idea for Ollida was born.

How it works

Ollida is a one-stop shop for K-pop fans, catered to the foreign demographic, and offers three main functionalities:

  • Aggregates various metrics such as Digital Single Sales, Album Sales, Social Media Points for K-pop Music Award Shows
  • Predicts nominees’ rankings in accordance to the chart system breakdowns
  • In-app marketplace connecting buyers & sellers of K-pop music albums

NodeJS, Express, PostgreSQL, MapBox GL JS
CJ Lim, Jackie Xiao, Zachary Lim
Background

My friends and I recently took part in the Singapore leg of the AngelHack Global Hackathon, of which the theme was seamless technology.

Our proposal

As lovers of hawker food, we realized something very troubling: while we can easily find all the Din Tai Fungs in Singapore, we do not know where all the hawker centres, kopitiams, and food courts are.

hawkersaurus crowd-sources data and reviews on hawker stalls to recreate Yelp  —  just for hawker stalls. A double-edged sword, hawkersaurus not only provides a fun, social and seamless experience for food lovers to discover hawker stalls, but also showcases and creates an online presence for hawkers, who are not easily found on mainstream restaurant discovery platforms such as Google Places, Yelp and TripAdvisor. On the app, hawkers can also engage in sponsored advertising.

JoJob

MERN: MongoDB, Express, React, NodeJS
Inspiration

At the end of every school year, college students need to move out of the dorm for the summer holidays. Usually, my friends and I share a storage unit, start planning early on to get the best storage unit rental prices, and settle our own transportation. We typically end up saving 70-80% of the prices charged by storage companies.

The entire logistics planning process for the group is pretty cumbersome, and has always been entirely managed by my friend, Joseph.

Inspired by Joseph (a.k.a Jo), JoJob eases the storage & moving logistics management process, catered to groups of people sharing storage units.

How it works
1. Log automatically divides up the total cost evenly when you add/delete an entity, or add a storage unit / rental truck. Download as Excel Spreadsheet.
2. Search for a storage unit, select unit size and order
3. Clicking 'Add to Log' will automatically update the log
4. Search for a rental truck, select pickup date
5. Clicking 'Add to Log' will automatically update the log
6. Layout planning for storage unit and rental truck
