Chain Reaction

Source Code
Demo (Warning: Sound included)
HTML5, CSS3, JavaScript
Inspiration

Having observed a few friends who are addicted to social media games, I have realized that the nature of gaming addition is cyclical: many turn to online games for momentary stress relief, unknowingly fall into addiction, become increasingly withdrawn from other domains of their lives, and consequently dive deeper into their addiction.

I wanted to create a simple game that provides a quick and effective outlet to relieve stress, yet limits the likelihood of addiction.

Gameplay

Place the target anywhere on the canvas, and capture as many balls as possible. At any given level, you need to capture a minimum number of balls in order to advance to the next level.

The score increment starts with 10, and increases by a multiple of 10 with each successive collision. The larger the chain reaction you create, the more points you score!

Video
Design

Some of my key design features were:

  • Requires minimal effort: player only makes 1 move per round
  • Fun but not too addictive: limit number of levels to 5 to reduce the time of a given playing session
  • Aestheically pleasing: integrate colorful designs and sound effects in the game
  • Blend of luck and strategy: balls will move randomly on the screen to add an element of luck and spontaneity, but player has to strategically position target to capture as many balls as possible

I persistently struggle with two things: 1) finding the time to read voraciously and 2) keeping my writing short and sweet (as they say, KISS). The former is a habit I really want to cultivate, and the latter is an improvement I want to make to my writing skills.

I decided to design a text summarizer can summarize both web articles and textual content.

Background

My friends and I recently took part in the Park of the Future Hackathon in Singapore organized by TNB Ventures & Ascendas-Singbridge. The theme was on envisioning a model for reinventing future business parks, to improve the experiences and operations of both tenants and landlords.

We emerged the winner of the 2nd Challenge — ‘Refining Residents’ Experience’ — and came in 3rd overall! It was a ton of fun, and a great learning experience as a rookie hacker.

Our proposal

We started by asking: what do business parks need, what do their tenants want? We talked to a few stakeholders, and they told us that they want a vibrant space that attracts innovative professionals, a co-space that fosters co-creation and synergies. We gathered that what the business park of today is missing is this community, and the spontaneous burst of life.

We designed CookieTalk, a platform for tenants to organize and attend spontaneous, pop-up events within business parks. We want to create a culture of ground-up self-organisation: with CookieTalk, any resident can create their own event — and these are meant to be spontaneous, pop-up events — not centrally organised workshops or conferences.

Design

We designed an interface similar to PokemonGo's on which tenants can discover pop-up events within the business park, anytime during the day. Like PokemonGo, CookieTalk provides a digital overlay over a physical space.

Next Steps

We're looking to integrate "interest groups" to connect users/residents to each other. In the longer term, we're looking to expand our target demographic to the general public and nearby institutions.

We also think that from a management perspective, the user information generated from CookieTalk activities may be useful in informing business parks on behavioural trends among their residents.

Jian Hao Tan, Isa Tokunaga
Background

K-pop (abbreviation for Korean pop) has amassed an enormous and growing fanbase globally. In Southeast Asia where I come from, K-pop has taken the region by storm over the past two decades.

Today, there are six main music shows in Korea—SBS’s Inkigayo, MBC’s Show! Music Core, KBS’s Music Bank, Mnet’s M! Countdown, MBC Music’s Show Champion and SBS MTV’s The Show—each designated for one day of the week, from Tuesday to Sunday. These music shows usually crown a “winning artist of the week,” determined by a chart system that focuses heavily on digital sales, retail sales, and social media popularity.

For example, here's the chart system breakdown for the M! Countdown Weekly Music Awards:

M! Countdown Chart System

Inspiration

The idea of Ollida came about when my teammate Isa related a complaint from her former colleague. Her friend, a huge K-pop fan, wanted to track her idol groups’ standings in the days leading up to the winning announcements, but had to manually visit the different sources of metrics, a process she found tedious and troublesome. Even if she did manage to find the relevant sites, many of the sites were in Korean, which she could not understand.

We discovered a huge pain point: there is currently no aggregator in the market for these statistics. Non-Korean speaking international K-pop fans also face a huge language barrier, since many of these websites are in Korean.

Upon further research, we also noted an interesting phenomenon: these music award shows exploit the psychology of fandom to build buzz and create hype. As Billboard describes, the “embrace of social media has played a large role in the way fans rally around their faves.”

The chart system's heavy weightage on Digital/Physical Sales as well as Social Media Points has given rise to a movement of "breaking records" whereby fans race to boost YouTube offical music video views, and as crazy as it sounds, repeatedly purchase digital albums through platforms such as 1) Bugs! 2) genie 3) melon.

How it works

Ollida is a one-stop shop for K-pop fans, catered to the foreign demographic, and offers a two-fold functuality:

  • Aggregates various metrics such as Digital Single Sales, Album Sales, Social Media Points for K-pop Music Award Shows, and predicts nominees’ rankings in accordance to the chart system breakdowns
  • In-app marketplace connecting buyers and sellers of K-pop music albums
